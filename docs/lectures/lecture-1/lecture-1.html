Lecture 1: Thermodynamic Properties

Process Modeling and Simulation

Published

Jun 16, 2026

Learning objectives

By the end of this lecture, students should be able to:

  • Identify common thermodynamic properties used in process modeling and simulation.
  • Distinguish between extensive and intensive thermodynamic properties.
  • Explain why state functions are important in process modeling.
  • Explain why enthalpy and Gibbs free energy are important in process models.

1. Thermodynamic properties

Thermodynamics is a macroscopic science. It describes how observable properties of a system change and determines the direction of processes, equilibrium states, and energy exchanges without referring explicitly to individual molecules.

The main thermodynamic properties used are summarized below.

Extensive property Intensive property Property
– \(P\) Pressure
– \(T\) Temperature
\(V\) \(v\) Volume
\(U\) \(u\) Internal energy
\(S\) \(s\) Entropy
\(H = U + PV\) \(h = u + Pv\) Enthalpy
\(G = H - TS\) \(g = h - Ts\) Gibbs free energy

These properties can be classified as:

  • Measurable properties: directly obtained from instruments, such as \(T\), \(P\), and \(V\).
  • Fundamental properties: appear in the basic laws and balances, such as \(U\) and \(S\).
  • Derived properties: convenient combinations of other properties, such as \(H = U + PV\) and \(G = H - TS\).

Intensive molar properties are obtained from extensive properties using

\[ x = \frac{X}{n_T} \]

where \(X\) is an extensive property and \(n_T\) is the total number of moles.

NoteDoes temperature measure the total energy contained in a system?
Show answer

Answer: No. If temperature measured the total energy contained in a system, then we would not need separate energy properties such as internal energy, \(U\). This is a common misconception.

Temperature does not measure the total energy contained in a system because total energy also depends on the amount of material present. Instead, temperature is related to the average kinetic energy associated with molecular motion, but it does not include all contributions to the energy of a system.

In microscopic terms, molecules are constantly moving through translation, rotation, and vibration. A higher temperature generally corresponds to more energetic molecular motion on average. For an ideal gas, this relationship is especially direct because temperature is proportional to the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules. However, the total energy of a real system also includes other contributions, such as intermolecular interactions and chemical bonding.

For now, we will treat thermodynamic properties as symbols. Their physical meaning and definitions will be discussed in more detail later.

2.Independent variables

In practice, thermodynamic properties of mixtures are usually expressed in terms of variables that are easy to specify and measure: temperature \(T\), pressure \(P\), volume \(V\), and the mole amounts \({n_k}\) of each component.

Conventionally, extensive properties are written as

\[ S = S(T,P,{n_k}), \qquad H = H(T,P,{n_k}), \qquad G = G(T,P,{n_k}), \qquad U = U(T,V,{n_k}). \]

The corresponding intensive, or molar, properties are written as

\[ s = s(T,P,{z_k}), \qquad h = h(T,P,{z_k}), \qquad g = g(T,P,{z_k}), \qquad u = u(T,V,{z_k}). \]

Here, \(z_k\) is the mole fraction of component \(k\):

\[ z_k = \frac{n_k}{\sum_k n_k}. \]

For a pure-component system, or for any system in which the composition is fixed, we usually drop the explicit dependence on \({n_k}\) or \({z_k}\). In that case, we simply write extensive properties as \(X(T,P)\) or \(X(T,V)\), and the corresponding molar properties as \(x(T,P)\) or \(x(T,V)\).

3. State functions

Thermodynamic properties are state functions. This means that their values depend only on the current equilibrium state of the system, specified for example by \(T\), \(P\), and \({n_k}\), and not on how the system reached that state.

If a system goes from state 1 to state 2, the change in a state function \(X\) is

\[ \Delta X = X_2 - X_1. \]

This value of \(\Delta X\) is the same for any path connecting state 1 and state 2, whether the path is reversible, irreversible, fast, slow, or otherwise.

Graphically, for a state function \(X\), we have

\[ \oint dX = 0. \]

Around any closed cycle, the system returns to the same state. Therefore, the value of the state function also returns to its original value.

NoteWhy are state functions important for process modeling?
Show answer Because thermodynamic properties are state functions, we can focus on the initial state and final state of each unit, or the input and output states, without specifying the detailed path followed inside the unit.

4.Absolute values and changes

For some thermodynamic properties, especially energy-related properties, the absolute value cannot usually be measured directly. Instead, their numerical values depend on the choice of a reference state. Therefore, in most thermodynamic calculations, only changes in these properties are physically relevant.

These properties include internal energy \(U\), enthalpy \(H\), and Gibbs free energy \(G\). In practice, we usually work with their changes, such as \(\Delta U\), \(\Delta H\), and \(\Delta G\) for finite processes, or with their differential forms, \(dU\), \(dH\), and \(dG\), for infinitesimal changes.

Entropy \(S\) is special. Unlike \(U\), \(H\), and \(G\), its absolute value can be assigned a physical meaning through the third law of thermodynamics. However, in many practical calculations, we still mainly use entropy changes, such as \(\Delta S\) or \(dS\).

NoteWhat is the third law?
Show answer The third law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of a perfect crystal at absolute zero temperature is exactly zero. This provides a reference point for assigning absolute values to entropy.

Thermodynamic laws are usually written in terms of changes in properties, not absolute numerical values. For example, the first law is commonly written in terms of changes in internal energy, such as \(\Delta U\) for a finite process or \(dU\) for an infinitesimal process. Similarly, the second law is written in terms of entropy changes, such as \(\Delta S\) or \(dS\).

Because the absolute values of some thermodynamic properties depend on the chosen reference, we assign numerical values by selecting a reference state. At this reference state, the value of the property is defined to be zero.

A common example is the use of standard enthalpies of formation:

\[ \Delta_f H^\circ_{298.15} \]

This quantity is defined relative to the standard reference state of the elements at \(298.15\ \text{K}\).

The important point is that changing the reference state only adds a constant to all property values. This constant may be positive or negative, but it does not affect property differences. For a thermodynamic property \(X\), suppose a new reference state changes every value by adding a constant \(C\). Then,

\[ \Delta X = (X_2 + C) - (X_1 + C) = X_2 - X_1. \]

Therefore, thermodynamic predictions based on changes in properties are unchanged by the choice of reference state. Calculations of heat, work, spontaneity, and equilibrium remain the same as long as the reference state is used consistently.

NoteDo not use values of thermodynamic properties without checking the reference state!
Show answer It is important to check the reference state when using values of thermodynamic properties from tables, databases, or software. If the reference state is not consistent with the one used in your calculations, you may need to adjust the property values by the difference in the reference states.

4. Important thermodynamic properties

4.1 Internal energy \(U\)

Internal energy is the macroscopic energy that reflects the microscopic contributions of molecules. These contributions include primarily the kinetic energy of molecular motion, such as translation, rotation, and vibration, and the potential energy associated with intermolecular forces and chemical bonding.

4.2 Enthalpy \(H\)

In process models of continuous-flow units, the enthalpy \(H(P,T,{n_k})\), or the molar enthalpy \(h(T,P,{z_k})\), appears naturally in the energy balance.

For an open system, or control volume, with inlet and outlet streams, the first law of thermodynamics can be written in terms of internal energy \(U\) plus the flow work \(PV\) required to push material into and out of the control volume. Grouping these contributions gives the definition of enthalpy:

\[ H = U + PV, \qquad h = u + Pv. \]

Mixing, reaction, and phase-change processes are often analyzed at constant pressure. Under this condition, the heat released or absorbed by the process is equal to the enthalpy change. This is why heats of mixing, heats of reaction, and latent heats of phase change are commonly reported as enthalpy changes:

Heat of reaction, \(\Delta_\mathrm{rxn}H\), is the enthalpy change between reactants and products at the same \(T\) and \(P\). It represents the heat released or absorbed by the reaction at constant pressure.

Heat of mixing, \(\Delta_\mathrm{mix}H\), is the enthalpy change when initially separated pure components are mixed to form a solution at a given \(T\) and \(P\). It measures the heat effect caused by intermolecular interactions in the mixture.

Heat of phase change, or latent heat, \(\Delta_\mathrm{latent}H\), is the enthalpy change associated with a phase transition at a given \(T\) and \(P\). For example, \(\Delta_\mathrm{vap}H\) represents the enthalpy of vaporization, and \(\Delta_\mathrm{fus}H\) represents the enthalpy of fusion or melting.

4.3 Entropy \(S\)

Entropy measures how dispersed energy is within a system. It was introduced to describe the direction of spontaneous processes. According to the second law of thermodynamics, a process can occur only if the total entropy of the universe, meaning the system plus the surroundings, increases.

Entropy is also closely linked to thermodynamic equilibrium. For an isolated system with no mass transfer, no heat or work exchange with the surroundings, and a rigid boundary so that \(V\) is constant, the equilibrium state is the one that maximizes the entropy \(S\) subject to the constraints \(U\), \(V\), and \({n_k}\).

4.4 Gibbs free energy \(G\)

Gibbs free energy \(G(P,T,{n_k})\), or molar Gibbs free energy \(g(P,T,{z_k})\), is named in honour of Josiah Willard Gibbs (1839–1903), an American scientist who developed statistical mechanics and laid the foundations of modern chemical thermodynamics and the Gibbs free energy concept.

The change in Gibbs free energy, \(G - G_{\mathrm{min}}\), represents the maximum portion of a system’s energy that is available to do useful non-\(PV\) work at constant \(T\) and \(P\). Examples include electrical work in a fuel cell, chemical reactions, and phase changes. This is why it is called a “free” energy.

Thus, \(G\) is the energy available to drive reactions, phase changes, mass transfer, and other material transformations at constant \(T\) and \(P\). This driving force is exhausted when \(G\) reaches its minimum value, \(G_{\mathrm{min}}\).

Application 1: Criterion for spontaneity and material equilibrium

For a system at constant \(T\) and \(P\), already in mechanical and thermal equilibrium and capable of doing only \(PV\) work, the second law leads to

\[ dG = dH - TdS \le 0. \]

Therefore:

  • \(dG < 0\) for a spontaneous change at fixed \(T\) and \(P\).
  • \(dG = 0\), or equivalently \(G = G_{\mathrm{min}}\), at material equilibrium.
  • At material equilibrium, no further spontaneous change is possible at fixed \(T\) and \(P\).

Application 2: Generating other thermodynamic properties

Gibbs free energy is a central thermodynamic potential, especially in process simulation. Many key properties can be obtained from its derivatives. For example,

\[ S = -\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial T}\right)_{P,{n_k}}, \qquad V = \left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial P}\right)_{T,{n_k}}. \]

Also,

\[ H = G + TS. \]

Using the derivative relationship for entropy,

\[ H = G - T\left(\frac{\partial G}{\partial T}\right)_{P,{n_k}}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ H = -T^2 \left( \frac{\partial (G/T)}{\partial T} \right)_{P,{n_k}}. \]

Therefore, if we know \(G(T,P,{n_k})\) and how it changes with \(T\) and \(P\), then \(S\), \(V\), and \(H\) can be obtained from it. Phase equilibria, reaction equilibria, and many other thermodynamic calculations can then be developed from the Gibbs free energy.

NoteWhy is Gibbs free energy model important for process modeling?
Show answer This will become more obvious later, but many process models are developed from the Gibbs free energy. If an expression for \(G\) is known, then other thermodynamic properties can be derived from it, and the necessary calculations for process simulation can be performed. For this reason, \(G\) is one of the central thermodynamic potentials.
NoteHow about the Helmholtz free energy \(A\)?
Show answer

If you have seen the Helmholtz free energy,

\[ A = U - TS, \]

in other thermodynamics courses, you may wonder how it differs from the Gibbs free energy and why we do not use it as often in chemical engineering.

The Helmholtz free energy \(A\) and the Gibbs free energy \(G\) are closely related thermodynamic potentials. The main difference is the conditions under which each potential is most convenient. The Helmholtz free energy is most useful for analyzing systems at constant temperature and constant volume, such as closed systems with rigid boundaries.

In chemical engineering and process simulation, however, we often analyze systems at constant temperature and constant pressure. These conditions are more directly connected to many practical process calculations, such as phase equilibrium and reaction equilibrium. Therefore, the Gibbs free energy \(G\) is usually more convenient for the thermodynamic calculations used in process simulation.
